**A randomized trial published in *Nature Medicine* on May 13, 2026 found that adults who took a daily pasteurized *Akkermansia muciniphila* MucT supplement for 24 weeks after dieting regained only 1.2 kg, versus 3.2 kg on placebo, a 63% reduction in weight regain ([Nature Medicine, 2026](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04394-7)).** It is the first well-powered human trial to show that a single gut bacterium can meaningfully blunt the post-diet "rebound" that wrecks most weight-loss efforts. Here is what the trial actually showed, what *Akkermansia* does inside your gut, and what to do (and not do) with this finding in 2026.

## Key takeaways

- In a 24-week trial of 90 adults who had lost at least 8% of their body weight, **the Akkermansia group regained 1.2 ± 0.7 kg vs 3.2 ± 0.4 kg on placebo** (P = 0.012), per the [*Nature Medicine* paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04394-7).
- About **40% of the Akkermansia group kept losing weight during the maintenance phase**, versus roughly 5% on placebo.
- The supplement was **pasteurized, not live** — heat-killed cells of the MucT strain. A 2019 *Nature Medicine* study by Cani et al. found the [pasteurized form actually outperformed live bacteria](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-019-0495-2) on metabolic markers.
- *Akkermansia muciniphila* lives in the **mucus layer of your colon** and helps maintain the gut barrier, supporting roughly 1–4% of a healthy gut microbiome.
- **You cannot copy the trial dose with food.** *Akkermansia* is not in yogurt, kefir, or kombucha. But a fiber-rich, polyphenol-rich diet does feed the *Akkermansia* you already have.

## Why post-diet weight regain is the real obesity problem

Losing weight is hard. *Keeping it off* is the part almost nobody talks about, and it is where most diets collapse. Long-term follow-up studies consistently find that **most people regain at least half of lost weight within two years and roughly 80% within five years**, regardless of whether the original diet was low-carb, low-fat, intermittent-fasting, or pharmaceutical.

The biology pushes back. Caloric restriction lowers resting metabolic rate, raises hunger hormones like ghrelin, and shifts fat tissue toward a more inflammatory, insulin-resistant state. The body acts like it has been starved and works to rebuild the fat stores it lost.

A gut bacterium that takes the edge off that rebound is genuinely interesting. That is what this trial set out to test.

## What the May 2026 trial did

The study, published in *Nature Medicine* on May 13, 2026, was a double-blind, placebo-controlled randomized trial run by researchers at Maastricht University Medical Center+, the University of Copenhagen, Wageningen University & Research, and The Akkermansia Company.

The protocol had two phases:

1. **Weight-loss phase (8 weeks).** All 90 adults with overweight or obesity followed a low-energy diet of 800–900 kcal/day until they had lost at least 8% of their body weight.
2. **Maintenance phase (24 weeks).** Participants who hit the 8% target were randomized to take either a daily oral dose of pasteurized *A. muciniphila* MucT or a matching placebo, alongside ad libitum healthy eating. Body weight, body composition, glucose tolerance, and adipose-tissue biology were tracked.

The headline result: at the end of 24 weeks, the MucT group had regained **1.2 ± 0.7 kg**, while the placebo group had regained **3.2 ± 0.4 kg**. The difference was statistically significant (P = 0.012). About **40% of MucT participants continued to lose weight** during the maintenance phase, versus only about **5% of placebo participants**.

Secondary readouts showed that the Akkermansia group had favorable shifts in adipose tissue — increased activity in pathways tied to energy metabolism and reduced inflammatory and immune signalling — consistent with a "less metabolically stuck" fat state. No serious adverse events linked to the treatment were reported.

## What *Akkermansia muciniphila* actually does in your gut

*Akkermansia muciniphila* is a strictly anaerobic, mucin-degrading bacterium first described in 2004. It lives in the mucus layer that coats your large intestine — the slick barrier between the bacterial party in your colon and your own epithelial cells. In healthy adults, it accounts for roughly 1–4% of the gut microbiome.

Its job is unusual. While most gut bacteria ferment dietary fiber, *Akkermansia* mostly eats **mucin**, the glycoprotein your own gut secretes. As it eats mucin, it stimulates your epithelial cells to make more, which **thickens and reinforces the gut barrier** rather than thinning it.

Two downstream effects matter for metabolism:

- **Tighter gut barrier.** Less bacterial endotoxin (LPS) leaks into circulation, which means lower low-grade systemic inflammation — a known driver of insulin resistance. For background on barrier function, see our piece on [whether leaky gut is real or a myth](/poopcheck-blog/leaky-gut-real-or-myth/).
- **Cross-feeding the wider microbiome.** Broken-down mucin sugars feed other beneficial bacteria, supporting microbial diversity without you eating anything extra. It is similar to the supporting role we covered for the [CAG-170 health-marker bacterium](/poopcheck-blog/cag-170-gut-bacteria-health-marker/).

Across observational studies, people with obesity, type 2 diabetes, inflammatory bowel disease, and metabolic syndrome consistently have **lower** *Akkermansia* abundance than healthy controls. The 2026 trial is one of the first to show that adding it back can move a hard clinical outcome.

## Why "pasteurized" Akkermansia is the surprising winner

The intuitive expectation with a probiotic is that live bacteria should work better than dead ones. *Akkermansia* breaks that intuition.

In the original 2019 proof-of-concept trial led by Patrice Cani's group, also in *Nature Medicine*, researchers found that **pasteurized** *A. muciniphila* — heat-killed cells — actually improved insulin sensitivity, lowered cholesterol, and reduced inflammatory markers more than the live bacterium did ([Depommier et al., 2019](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-019-0495-2)). The active component appears to be a protein called Amuc_1100 on the bacterium's outer membrane, which signals to the gut barrier and immune system independently of whether the bug is alive.

That makes pasteurized *Akkermansia* a **postbiotic** by the ISAPP definition — a "preparation of inanimate microorganisms" that confers a health benefit. For the broader category, see our explainer on [postbiotics](/poopcheck-blog/postbiotics-explained/) and the wider piece on [probiotics vs prebiotics](/poopcheck-blog/probiotics-vs-prebiotics/).

There are practical upsides: pasteurized cells are more shelf-stable, easier to dose consistently, and carry no theoretical bacteremia risk for immunocompromised users.

## How to support *Akkermansia* you already have

The trial used a specific pasteurized MucT strain. You cannot buy it at a grocery store, and it is **not** present in yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, or kombucha — common probiotic foods all rely on *Lactobacillus*, *Bifidobacterium*, *Acetobacter*, or yeast species, not *Akkermansia*.

What you *can* do is feed the *Akkermansia* already living in your gut. The published patterns are reasonably consistent ([Gavi explainer](https://www.gavi.org/vaccineswork/mucus-loving-gut-bacteria-important-health-heres-how-keep-it-happy)):

- **Eat polyphenol-rich foods.** Cranberries, pomegranate, grapes, green tea, and dark chocolate have all been linked to higher *Akkermansia* levels in human studies.
- **Get diverse plant fibers daily.** *Akkermansia* itself prefers mucin, but a healthy mucus layer depends on a well-fed wider microbiome producing short-chain fatty acids. See [fiber and stool consistency](/poopcheck-blog/fiber-and-stool-consistency/) for the practical fiber targets.
- **Include fish oil, flax, and olive oil.** Omega-3 and monounsaturated fats have been linked to thicker mucus layers in animal studies and modest *Akkermansia* increases in humans.
- **Avoid prolonged very-low-fiber diets.** A 2023 *Nature Microbiology* paper showed *Akkermansia* can flip from protective to harmful when starved of fiber, because it strips the mucus barrier without replacing it.

The simpler frame: a high-plant-diversity, polyphenol-rich Mediterranean-style diet has the best evidence for supporting *Akkermansia*. There is no specific "Akkermansia food" the way garlic is a fructan source for *Bifidobacterium*.

## Is an Akkermansia supplement worth it in 2026?

A pasteurized *Akkermansia* MucT supplement from The Akkermansia Company has been available in Europe since 2022 and in the US since 2024 under novel-food authorisations. The 2026 trial is the strongest evidence to date that it can do what the marketing claims, in the specific context of post-diet weight maintenance.

A few honest caveats before you reach for it:

- **The trial was funded in part by the manufacturer** (The Akkermansia Company is listed among the contributing institutions). Independent replication is the gold standard, and it has not yet happened.
- **The effect size is modest in absolute terms.** A 2-kg difference over 24 weeks is real but not life-changing.
- **The trial tested it after an aggressive 800–900 kcal/day diet.** Whether the same benefit applies after gentler weight loss, GLP-1 medications like Ozempic and Wegovy, or weight loss surgery has not been demonstrated.
- **Baseline matters.** Earlier work suggests benefits are larger in people who started with *lower* native *Akkermansia* levels — a pattern this trial also picked up. Without testing, you do not know where you stand. Our piece on [whether gut microbiome testing is worth it](/poopcheck-blog/gut-microbiome-testing-worth-it/) covers what the consumer tests can and cannot tell you.
- **It is not a weight-loss drug.** It blunts regain after losing weight by other means. It will not replace dietary changes, GLP-1 medications, or exercise. For how GLP-1s reshape gut motility specifically, see [Ozempic and bowel changes](/poopcheck-blog/ozempic-and-bowel-changes/).

For most people, the rational next step is food first, supplements second. If you have just finished a structured weight-loss phase and want to extend the literature directly, an *Akkermansia* MucT supplement is one of the few next-generation gut interventions with a clean randomized trial behind it.

## What it means for your daily stool tracking

The 2026 study is a research milestone, but the everyday signal of a healthy gut barrier and balanced microbiome has not changed. The classic markers — [Bristol Type 3–4 stools](/poopcheck-blog/bristol-stool-chart-types-explained/), [healthy brown color](/poopcheck-blog/what-your-poop-color-means/), steady frequency, low gas, no urgency — reflect the *function* of the whole gut community, which is what *Akkermansia* research consistently points to as the thing that actually matters.

If you start a pasteurized *Akkermansia* supplement, the most useful at-home outcome to track is not "did my gut microbiome change" (you cannot see that without sequencing) but **whether your daily stool consistency and weight trajectory shifted**. ## FAQ

### Can I get *Akkermansia muciniphila* from food?

No. *Akkermansia* is not present in any common probiotic food — not yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, kombucha, or miso. The only way to take it directly is as a supplement of the pasteurized MucT strain. You can, however, support the *Akkermansia* already in your gut with polyphenol-rich foods (cranberries, pomegranate, green tea, dark chocolate), omega-3 fats, and diverse plant fibers.

### Why does pasteurized Akkermansia work better than live?

The active compound appears to be a membrane protein called Amuc_1100, which signals to gut-barrier cells and immune cells regardless of whether the bacterium is alive. Pasteurization keeps that protein intact while killing the cells. The 2019 *Nature Medicine* proof-of-concept trial found pasteurized cells improved metabolic markers more reliably than live cells.

### How much weight did the 2026 trial participants regain?

After 24 weeks of maintenance, the pasteurized *Akkermansia* MucT group regained an average of **1.2 kg**, compared with **3.2 kg** in the placebo group. About 40% of the supplement group continued losing weight during the maintenance phase, versus around 5% on placebo.

### Will *Akkermansia* help me lose weight in the first place?

The 2026 trial did not test that. Participants lost weight on a low-calorie diet, *then* started the supplement to maintain the loss. There is no good human-trial evidence that *Akkermansia* drives weight loss in people who have not already lost weight by other means.

### Is *Akkermansia muciniphila* safe?

In the 2026 trial, no serious adverse events were linked to treatment. The 2019 proof-of-concept trial also reported a clean safety profile over 3 months. As a pasteurized (heat-killed) cell preparation, it carries no theoretical bacteremia risk. People who are pregnant, severely immunocompromised, or taking immunosuppressants should consult a clinician before using any novel-food microbial supplement.

### How does it compare to a regular probiotic?

Pasteurized *Akkermansia* is technically a **postbiotic**, not a probiotic — there are no live cells. The mechanism (gut-barrier signalling via Amuc_1100) is different from how *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium* probiotics work (transient colonization, short-chain fatty-acid production). The evidence base for *Akkermansia* on weight maintenance is now stronger than for any conventional probiotic on the same outcome.

## The bottom line

The May 2026 *Nature Medicine* trial is the first well-designed human evidence that a single gut bacterium can blunt post-diet weight regain. The effect — about 2 kg less regain over 24 weeks, with 40% of supplement users continuing to lose weight — is modest but real. It is not a weight-loss drug, and it is not in your yogurt. But it does sharpen the picture of the gut barrier as a metabolic organ, and it gives anyone finishing a weight-loss phase a defensible next-generation tool to consider. The everyday gut-health basics did not change: feed your microbes a wide variety of plants, mind the polyphenols, and track what your stool is telling you about whether any of it is actually working.

Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) is an excess of bacteria — usually colon-type bacteria — living in the small intestine, where they don't belong in those numbers. The classic symptoms are bloating, gas, abdominal discomfort, and altered bowel habits, which overlap heavily with [irritable bowel syndrome](/poopcheck-blog/ibs-plague-of-the-millenium/). The ACG's first-line treatment is the antibiotic rifaximin, and a meta-analysis of more than 1,300 patients put the overall symptomatic response rate at about **70.8%** ([Pimentel et al., ACG 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32023228/)). The complication is that breath testing — the test most patients get — is far from a clean yes/no, and recurrence after a successful course is the rule rather than the exception.

## Key takeaways

- SIBO is defined as **excessive bacteria in the small intestine** producing symptoms; pooled prevalence in IBS patients is around **38%**, versus roughly 9–21% in healthy controls ([Shah et al., systematic review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29761234/)).
- The **North American Consensus** defines a positive hydrogen breath test as a **rise of ≥20 ppm by 90 minutes** and methane positivity as **≥10 ppm at any point** ([Rezaie et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28323273/)).
- Glucose breath testing has pooled **sensitivity of 54.5% and specificity of 83.2%**; lactulose is worse, at **42% and 70.6%** ([Losurdo et al., 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6955189/)). False positives are common, especially with lactulose.
- **Rifaximin 550 mg three times daily for 14 days** is the ACG-recommended first-line regimen ([ACG 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32023228/)). Methane-positive patients (now called intestinal methanogen overgrowth, or IMO) often get rifaximin **plus neomycin**.
- **Recurrence is common.** In one prospective study, **43.7% of successfully treated patients relapsed within 9 months**, with PPI use, older age, and prior appendectomy as risk factors ([Lauritano et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18802998/)).

## What SIBO actually is

The small intestine isn't sterile, but it's much less densely colonized than the colon. Coordinated motility, gastric acid, bile, pancreatic enzymes, and the ileocecal valve all keep small-bowel bacterial counts low. When any of those defenses fail, colon-type bacteria migrate upstream and ferment carbohydrates in the small intestine instead of the colon. That fermentation produces hydrogen, methane, or hydrogen sulfide locally, which is where the symptoms come from.

The 2020 American College of Gastroenterology guideline defines SIBO as **the presence of excessive numbers of bacteria in the small bowel causing gastrointestinal symptoms** ([Pimentel et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32023228/)). The aspirate threshold in the same guideline is **≥10³ colony-forming units per milliliter** of jejunal fluid — lower than the older 10⁵ threshold, which reflected colonic contamination rather than true overgrowth.

A 2024 clinical practice update jointly endorsed by the European and American neurogastroenterology societies pushed the field even further: it argued that the "SIBO" label as currently used probably lumps together several distinct microbiome disturbances, and that breath testing alone can't reliably separate them ([Kashyap et al., *Neurogastroenterology & Motility* 2024](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/nmo.14817)). In other words, "SIBO" is a useful clinical concept but a noisier diagnosis than the marketing around breath testing suggests.

## Symptoms — and why they look exactly like IBS

The core SIBO symptom cluster is:

- **Bloating and abdominal distension**, often worse after meals (especially carbs)
- **Excess gas / belching / flatulence**
- **Abdominal discomfort or cramping**
- **Altered bowel habits** — diarrhea, constipation, or alternating
- **Steatorrhea** (greasy, [floating stool](/poopcheck-blog/what-causes-floating-stool/)) in more severe cases, from fat malabsorption
- **Fatigue, brain fog**, and in long-standing cases, **B12 deficiency** or iron deficiency

This list is almost identical to IBS, which is why the two get confused. The pooled prevalence of a positive SIBO breath test in IBS patients is about **38%** (95% CI 32–44%) versus **9–21% in healthy controls** ([Shah et al., systematic review and meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29761234/)). IBS-D (diarrhea-predominant) skews even higher. But "positive breath test" isn't the same as "SIBO causing your symptoms," which is exactly the diagnostic mess the 2024 update was responding to.

If your symptoms feel familiar from our [stool smell guide](/poopcheck-blog/stool-smell-what-it-means/) or you've noticed [pale, oily, floating stool](/poopcheck-blog/what-causes-floating-stool/), SIBO is worth raising with your clinician — but so is celiac disease, exocrine pancreatic insufficiency, bile-acid malabsorption, and giardiasis, all of which mimic it.

## Who gets SIBO

Anything that disrupts the normal defenses against bacterial migration raises risk. The biggest categories:

- **Motility disorders** — gastroparesis, scleroderma, diabetic enteropathy, post-surgical ileus, hypothyroidism. Slow transit gives bacteria time to set up shop.
- **Anatomic changes** — small-bowel diverticula, strictures (from Crohn's, radiation, or surgery), blind loops, ileocecal valve resection, gastric bypass.
- **Reduced gastric acid** — long-term PPI use, atrophic gastritis, post-gastrectomy. Acid is the gut's first antibacterial barrier.
- **Immunodeficiency** — common variable immunodeficiency, IgA deficiency, advanced HIV.
- **Connective tissue disease** — scleroderma is the classic high-prevalence condition; SIBO rates run well above 50% in established systemic sclerosis.

PPI use deserves special mention because so many people take one. Long-term PPI exposure is one of the strongest replicable risk factors for SIBO in observational data and was an independent predictor of recurrence in the Lauritano follow-up study ([Lauritano et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18802998/)).

## How SIBO is diagnosed

There are three tools, none perfect.

### 1. Breath testing (hydrogen, methane, ± hydrogen sulfide)

The patient drinks a measured carbohydrate — typically **75 g of glucose** or **10 g of lactulose** — and breath samples are collected every 15–20 minutes for 2–3 hours. The lab measures hydrogen and methane (and, in newer 3-gas tests, hydrogen sulfide). Per the **North American Consensus**, a **rise in hydrogen of ≥20 ppm above baseline by 90 minutes** is positive for SIBO; a **methane level of ≥10 ppm at any time** is positive for intestinal methanogen overgrowth ([Rezaie et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28323273/)).

The catch is performance. A 2020 systematic review and meta-analysis pooled the diagnostic accuracy of breath tests against jejunal aspirate as the reference standard:

- **Glucose breath test:** sensitivity **54.5%**, specificity **83.2%**
- **Lactulose breath test:** sensitivity **42.0%**, specificity **70.6%**

Source: [Losurdo et al., *J Neurogastroenterol Motil* 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6955189/). Those are not great numbers. Lactulose in particular generates false positives because it can reach the cecum before the 90-minute window closes — what looks like a "small bowel" hydrogen rise is sometimes just normal colonic fermentation arriving early. The 2024 ESNM/ANMS update specifically cautioned against treating breath testing as a binary diagnostic and recommended interpreting results alongside symptoms and clinical context ([Kashyap et al., 2024](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/nmo.14817)).

### 2. Jejunal aspirate and culture

Endoscopically sampling small-bowel fluid and culturing it is the closest thing to a gold standard. The ACG threshold is **≥10³ CFU/mL** of jejunal aspirate. The downsides are obvious: it requires sedated upper endoscopy, contamination from the oropharynx is hard to fully avoid, and many overgrowth organisms are anaerobes that don't culture well. Aspirate is rarely the first test; it's reserved for diagnostic uncertainty or research.

### 3. Symptom + treatment trial

In primary care and many GI clinics, an empirical trial of rifaximin is sometimes used as a pragmatic "test." Response supports the diagnosis; non-response argues against. It's not elegant, but given the breath-test limitations, it's a real-world strategy.

## What hydrogen, methane, and hydrogen sulfide each mean

The gas profile actually matters for the phenotype.

- **Hydrogen-dominant SIBO** is the classic form, often associated with **diarrhea or mixed bowel habit**.
- **Methane-dominant overgrowth — now called intestinal methanogen overgrowth (IMO)** — correlates with **constipation**. Methanogens are technically archaea, not bacteria, and methane appears to slow intestinal transit, which is why the rebranding happened. The ACG recommends rifaximin **plus neomycin** for symptomatic methane-positive patients ([ACG 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32023228/)).
- **Hydrogen sulfide–dominant (now called intestinal sulfide overproduction, or ISO)** correlates with **diarrhea** and is often described by patients as producing a strong rotten-egg odor. A 2025 study in *Digestive Diseases and Sciences* confirmed that breath methane and hydrogen sulfide track with the relevant organisms in the small intestine ([Singer-Englar et al., 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10620-025-09156-y)). Three-gas tests (the Trio-Smart, for example) report all three.

Practically: a methane-positive patient with chronic constipation is a different clinical scenario from a hydrogen-positive patient with diarrhea, even though both might be labeled "SIBO" in casual conversation.

## How SIBO is treated

### Antibiotics — rifaximin first

The ACG 2020 guideline gives a conditional recommendation for **rifaximin 550 mg three times daily for 14 days** as first-line therapy for symptomatic SIBO. A pooled analysis of 32 studies and 1,331 patients reported an overall **symptomatic response rate of about 70.8%** with adverse-reaction rates around 4.6% ([ACG 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32023228/)). Rifaximin is poorly absorbed from the gut, which is why systemic side effects are uncommon.

For methane-positive cases (IMO), the response to rifaximin alone is weaker. Adding **neomycin 500 mg twice daily for 14 days** improves response in this subgroup, again per the ACG.

### Elemental diet

A 2- to 3-week course of an elemental formula (pre-digested protein, simple carbs, fats) starves the bacteria of fermentable substrate. Reported normalization rates in small studies are 80%+ after two weeks, but the diet is hard to tolerate, expensive, and short on large randomized data.

### Herbal antimicrobials

A 2014 retrospective single-center study suggested herbal combinations (berberine, oregano, allicin, neem) were roughly comparable to rifaximin in resolving breath-test SIBO. The evidence base is thin compared with rifaximin, but the option exists for patients who can't or won't take antibiotics. Treat any "natural cure" claims with the same skepticism you'd apply to any other off-label protocol.

### Treat the underlying cause

This is where most SIBO management fails. If the patient has untreated hypothyroidism, ongoing PPI use without indication, untreated scleroderma, or an adhesion causing a partial obstruction, the bacteria will come back. The ACG explicitly recommends evaluating and addressing the underlying predisposing condition where possible.

## Why SIBO comes back

Recurrence is the defining problem in SIBO management. In a prospective study of 80 patients who had a successful course of rifaximin, **12.6% relapsed at 3 months, 27.5% at 6 months, and 43.7% at 9 months** ([Lauritano et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18802998/)). Independent predictors of recurrence were **older age, prior appendectomy, and chronic PPI use**.

The implication is that "kill the bugs" is a partial solution at best. If the conditions that allowed the overgrowth in the first place — slow motility, low acid, anatomic abnormality — are still present, the niche refills. Maintenance strategies include prokinetics for motility-driven SIBO, periodic short courses of antibiotics for refractory cases, dietary changes (some patients do well on a [low-FODMAP approach](/poopcheck-blog/low-fodmap-diet-guide/) for symptom control, though it doesn't treat the overgrowth itself), and aggressive treatment of any underlying disease.

## FAQ

**Is SIBO the same as IBS?**
No, but they overlap. About 38% of IBS patients have a positive SIBO breath test ([Shah et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29761234/)). IBS is a symptom-based diagnosis (Rome IV criteria); SIBO is a microbial diagnosis. Treating SIBO can improve symptoms in some IBS patients, but a positive breath test alone doesn't reclassify IBS as something else.

**Which breath test is more accurate, glucose or lactulose?**
Glucose. Pooled sensitivity is 54.5% and specificity 83.2% against jejunal aspirate, versus 42% / 70.6% for lactulose ([Losurdo et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6955189/)). Lactulose generates more false positives because it can reach the cecum quickly and trigger a hydrogen rise that looks like small-bowel fermentation. Neither test is perfect.

**Will probiotics fix SIBO?**
The evidence is mixed and the mechanism is uncertain. Some randomized trials have shown modest benefit from specific strains, others have shown no effect or worsening. The 2020 ACG guideline does not recommend probiotics as first-line therapy. Our [probiotics vs prebiotics](/poopcheck-blog/probiotics-vs-prebiotics/) guide covers the broader evidence.

**Does the low-FODMAP diet treat SIBO?**
It treats the symptoms (less fermentable substrate = less gas) but doesn't eradicate the bacteria. About 70% of IBS patients respond symptomatically to low-FODMAP, but it's a symptom-management tool, not an antimicrobial. Use it alongside, not instead of, addressing the underlying cause.

**Why does my SIBO keep coming back?**
Because the predisposing condition usually isn't fixed by the antibiotic. PPI use, slow motility, anatomic abnormalities, and aging all raise recurrence risk; 43.7% of treated patients relapse within 9 months in the most-cited prospective series ([Lauritano et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18802998/)). Long-term management means treating the cause, not just the overgrowth.

**Should I get tested if I have bloating?**
If bloating is mild and intermittent and you don't have red-flag symptoms (weight loss, blood, anemia, nocturnal symptoms, fevers), starting with diet and lifestyle changes is reasonable. If symptoms are persistent, impair daily life, or don't respond to first-line measures, a clinician evaluation — which may include breath testing — is appropriate. Self-ordering a breath test without a plan for interpretation often creates more confusion than clarity.

## The bottom line

SIBO is real, but it's noisier than the marketing around it suggests. Breath testing has moderate sensitivity at best, false positives are common (especially with lactulose), and a positive test doesn't automatically mean "your symptoms are caused by SIBO." When the diagnosis fits, rifaximin works for about 70% of patients, with neomycin added for methane-positive cases. The harder problem is recurrence: nearly half of successfully treated patients relapse within nine months unless the underlying motility, anatomic, or medication-driven cause is addressed.

Tracking your stool — Bristol type, frequency, color, smell — gives you and your clinician a paper trail of how symptoms change with treatment.

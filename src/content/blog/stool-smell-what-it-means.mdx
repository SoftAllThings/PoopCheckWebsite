All stool smells — that's normal. The odor mostly comes from **hydrogen sulfide and other sulfur-containing gases** that gut bacteria produce when they ferment protein and sulfur-rich foods in the colon. The question worth asking isn't whether your poop smells, but whether it smells *unusually* bad, and whether anything else has changed at the same time. A sudden shift to a rancid, oily odor can point to fat malabsorption; a strong metallic or tarry smell can signal upper-GI bleeding; and a persistent foul stink that sticks around for weeks deserves a workup, not a candle.

## Key takeaways

- **Hydrogen sulfide** (the rotten-egg gas) is the dominant driver of stool odor, produced when colon bacteria ferment sulfur amino acids from animal protein ([Teigen et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10413438/)).
- **High-protein, low-fiber diets** push H₂S production up; resistant starch and fiber push it down — even with ~30% of calories from protein.
- A **rancid, oily, hard-to-flush** smell paired with pale or floating stool points to **steatorrhea** (fat malabsorption), per the [Cleveland Clinic steatorrhea guide](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/symptoms/24049-steatorrhea-fatty-stool).
- A **strong, metallic, tar-like** odor with black, sticky stool can mean **melena** — upper-GI bleeding, an emergency.
- Anecdotal "C. diff has a sweet smell" claims don't survive blinded testing: nurses scored a median **45% accuracy** in a controlled study ([Rao et al., 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3571629/)). Use *symptoms*, not smell, to decide when to call a doctor.

## What actually makes poop smell

Most of stool's odor profile traces back to a handful of volatile sulfur and nitrogen compounds released by colon bacteria:

- **Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S)** — the classic rotten-egg or sewer note.
- **Methanethiol and dimethyl sulfide** — pungent garlic-like sulfur compounds.
- **Indole and skatole** — produced from tryptophan; the "barnyard" smell at low concentrations and outright fecal at high concentrations.
- **Short-chain fatty acids** — acetate, propionate, butyrate; sour, sharp notes.
- **Ammonia** — from protein breakdown.

The single biggest variable is **how much sulfur-containing amino acid (methionine, cysteine) reaches the colon**. Animal protein is rich in both. When colon bacteria — especially sulfate-reducing species like *Desulfovibrio* and *Bilophila wadsworthia* — get enough sulfur substrate, they ferment it into H₂S. A 2023 review in *Current Opinion in Clinical Nutrition and Metabolic Care* found a clear positive relationship between dietary protein intake and H₂S production, and showed that **dietary fiber blunts that effect** even when protein stays high ([Teigen, Biruete & Khoruts 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10413438/)).

So a stinky day after a steakhouse dinner is a microbial story, not a health story. A stinky *month* is something else.

## Diet contributors: why your stool smells today

Before chasing a diagnosis, run through the everyday culprits.

**High-sulfur foods** feed the H₂S pathway without causing problems on their own: red meat, eggs, dairy, cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, Brussels sprouts, kale), garlic, onions, beer, and processed foods with sulfite preservatives. A single sulfur-heavy meal is enough to shift odor for a day or two, especially if your gut microbiome already runs sulfide-heavy.

**Low fiber, high animal protein.** Carnivore-style and "all-meat" eating patterns push sulfide production up because fiber is what normally redirects bacteria toward fermenting carbohydrates instead of protein. The 2023 review notes that adding **resistant starch and fiber** lowers H₂S even when protein intake stays at ~30% of calories. If your stool odor went sideways after cutting carbs, that's your prime suspect. See our guide on [fiber and stool consistency](/poopcheck-blog/fiber-and-stool-consistency/) for practical fiber targets.

**Lactose and FODMAP intolerance.** Undigested lactose, fructans, and polyols ferment in the colon and raise total gas production, including a fraction of sulfur gases. Smell tracks tightly with the trigger food and resolves when the food does. A structured elimination like the [low FODMAP protocol](/poopcheck-blog/low-fodmap-diet-guide/) is the cleanest way to identify what your gut tolerates.

**Alcohol and certain medications.** Alcohol shifts colonic fermentation. Iron supplements, sulfur-containing multivitamins, some antibiotics, and orlistat all change stool smell in predictable ways.

## When odor signals a real problem

Persistent foul smell paired with a change in stool form, color, or frequency is the pattern that matters.

### Steatorrhea: the rancid, oily smell

Steatorrhea is fat in the stool — a hallmark of **malabsorption**. Per the [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/symptoms/24049-steatorrhea-fatty-stool) and the [NIH StatPearls steatorrhea chapter](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK541055/), the stool typically:

- Smells **rancid, oily, or sickly sweet** — distinctly worse than usual
- Looks **pale, yellowish, or clay-colored**
- Floats and leaves an oily film on the water
- Is **bulky and hard to flush**

Diagnostic threshold: **more than 7 g of fat per 24 hours** on a 100 g/day fat diet, measured with a 72-hour stool fat collection. Common drivers are exocrine pancreatic insufficiency, celiac disease, Crohn's disease affecting the terminal ileum, and bile-related issues. We cover the buoyancy side of the picture in [why does my stool float?](/poopcheck-blog/what-causes-floating-stool/).

### Melena: the metallic, tarry smell

If the smell is best described as **metallic, burnt, or tar-like**, and the stool is **black, sticky, and clingy**, that pattern is **melena** — black stool from digested blood in the upper GI tract. Per the [Cleveland Clinic melena guide](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/symptoms/25058-melena), the foul odor is a byproduct of hemoglobin being broken down by stomach acid and gut bacteria into hematin. About 50 mL of blood or more in the upper GI tract is enough to produce it. This is an **ER-now** finding when paired with dizziness, vomiting blood, severe pain, or a racing heart — see our deep dive on [black stool causes](/poopcheck-blog/black-stool-causes/) for the full red-flag list.

### Infections: giardia, C. diff, and viral gastroenteritis

GI infections shift odor sharply, usually alongside frequent watery stools.

- **Giardiasis** classically produces **greasy, frothy, very foul-smelling diarrhea**, often with bloating and gas, after travel or contaminated-water exposure ([NHS giardiasis guide](https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/giardiasis/)).
- **C. difficile** is widely described as having a "sweet" or "barnyard" smell, but the evidence is weaker than nursing folklore suggests. In a blinded laboratory study, 18 nurses asked to identify C. diff samples by odor scored a **median 45% accuracy**, no better than chance ([Rao et al., 2013, *Clinical Infectious Diseases*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3571629/)). Diagnose C. diff by symptoms (watery diarrhea, recent antibiotics, fever, abdominal pain) and a stool toxin test, not by sniff test.
- **Viral gastroenteritis** produces watery, foul-smelling diarrhea that resolves within 2–3 days in healthy adults.

### IBD and SIBO

Chronic foul-smelling stool also shows up in inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis) and small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO). Both involve disrupted absorption and abnormal bacterial activity. Hydrogen-sulfide-dominant SIBO specifically presents with a **strong rotten-egg odor** and is sometimes tested with a 3-gas breath test. If you have [IBS](/poopcheck-blog/ibs-plague-of-the-millenium/)-pattern symptoms that haven't responded to standard care, ask your GI about SIBO testing.

## When to see a doctor

Most foul-smelling stool resolves on its own within a few days. Get clinical evaluation if any of these apply:

- Foul odor lasts **more than 2–3 weeks** or has clearly changed from your baseline
- Stool is also **pale, oily, floating, or bulky** (steatorrhea pattern)
- Stool is **black, tarry, sticky** with a **strong metallic smell** (possible melena — same-day or ER care)
- Bright red or maroon blood in the stool
- **Unintentional weight loss**, fatigue, or new iron-deficiency anemia
- **Persistent diarrhea** for more than 2 days, especially after travel or antibiotics
- Fever above 38.5 °C (101.3 °F) with the GI symptoms
- A family history of IBD, celiac disease, or colorectal cancer combined with new symptoms

## FAQ

**Why does my poop smell so bad all of a sudden?**
The most common reason is dietary: a meal heavy in red meat, eggs, dairy, garlic, onions, or cruciferous vegetables feeds sulfide-producing colon bacteria. Alcohol, new supplements, or a temporary GI bug can do the same. Sudden odor that resolves within a few days rarely needs a workup.

**Is foul-smelling stool a sign of cancer?**
Stool odor alone is not a reliable cancer signal. The cancer-related red flag is **bleeding** — bright red, maroon, or black tarry stool — sometimes paired with weight loss, anemia, or a change in bowel habit lasting weeks. Persistent foul odor *alongside* those signs is worth investigating, but odor by itself is much more likely to be diet, malabsorption, or infection.

**Does C. diff really have a distinctive smell?**
There's a long-standing belief that C. difficile stool smells uniquely sweet or barnyard-like. A 2013 blinded study in *Clinical Infectious Diseases* found nurses correctly identified C. diff samples about 45% of the time — no better than chance ([Rao et al., 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3571629/)). Use the clinical picture (recent antibiotics, watery diarrhea, fever) and stool testing to make the call.

**Why does my poop smell metallic?**
A metallic, tar-like odor with black, sticky stool is the textbook description of **melena** — bleeding in the upper GI tract that has been digested into hematin on its way out. This is a medical emergency, especially with lightheadedness, vomiting blood, or severe abdominal pain. Iron supplements can also turn stool dark, but the smell stays normal.

**Can probiotics or fiber make poop smell better?**
Often yes. Fiber and resistant starch shift colonic fermentation toward short-chain fatty acid production and away from sulfide production, which can noticeably reduce odor over a few weeks ([Teigen et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10413438/)). Probiotics are more variable; the strongest evidence is for specific strains in specific conditions, not generic "gut deodorizing." See our breakdown of [probiotics vs. prebiotics](/poopcheck-blog/probiotics-vs-prebiotics/).

**Is sweet-smelling poop dangerous?**
Sometimes, sometimes not. A vaguely sweet smell after a fruit-heavy meal is meaningless. A persistently sweet-rancid odor paired with greasy, pale, floating stool can fit steatorrhea. A "sickly sweet" or barnyard description has been attached to C. diff, but as above, smell isn't a reliable diagnostic. The deciding factor is what else is happening: diarrhea, weight change, recent antibiotics, blood, fever.

## The bottom line

Stool smell is mostly a microbiology readout — what you ate, who lives in your gut, and how those bugs ferment your last meal. Diet shifts can swing the odor for a day or two and don't need attention. The combinations that *do* need attention are the durable ones: rancid and oily for weeks (think malabsorption), metallic and tarry with black stool (think bleeding), or persistent foul stink with diarrhea, weight loss, or blood (think infection or IBD).

Stool color is one of the most immediate indicators of your digestive health. While most variations are completely normal and caused by diet, certain colors can signal underlying health conditions that warrant medical attention.

## What is a Normal Poop Color?

Normal stool color ranges from light to dark brown. This brown color comes from bilirubin, a pigment produced when red blood cells break down. Bile from your liver processes bilirubin as food moves through your digestive tract, giving stool its characteristic brown hue.

## Green Stool

Green poop is usually harmless. The most common causes include eating green leafy vegetables (spinach, kale), green food coloring, or iron supplements. It can also mean food moved through your large intestine too quickly for bile to fully break down.

**When to be concerned:** Persistent green stool without dietary explanation, especially when accompanied by diarrhea, could indicate an infection.

## Yellow Stool

Yellow, greasy, foul-smelling stool can indicate excess fat — a condition called steatorrhea. This may suggest your body isn't absorbing nutrients properly.

**Possible causes:** Celiac disease, chronic pancreatitis, or other malabsorption conditions. If you notice consistently yellow stool, consult your healthcare provider.

## Black Stool

Black or tarry stool (melena) can be caused by iron supplements, bismuth-containing medications (like Pepto-Bismol), or eating dark foods like black licorice or blueberries.

**When to be concerned:** Black, tarry stool with a foul smell could indicate upper gastrointestinal bleeding. Seek medical attention promptly if you haven't taken iron supplements or eaten dark-colored foods.

## Red Stool

Red stool is often caused by red-colored foods like beets, tomatoes, cranberries, or red food coloring.

**When to be concerned:** Bright red blood in stool could indicate hemorrhoids, anal fissures, or more serious conditions like inflammatory bowel disease or colorectal issues. Contact your doctor if you see blood in your stool.

## White or Clay-Colored Stool

Pale, white, or clay-colored stool indicates a lack of bile. This can be caused by certain medications (antacids, anti-diarrheal drugs) but may also suggest bile duct obstruction.

**When to be concerned:** Persistently pale stool should always be evaluated by a healthcare provider, as it can indicate liver or gallbladder problems.

## When to See a Doctor

Consult a healthcare professional if you notice:

- Black, tarry stool not explained by diet or medications
- Bright red blood in your stool
- Persistently white or clay-colored stool
- Any dramatic, unexplained color change lasting more than a few days
- Color changes accompanied by pain, fever, or weight loss

## Key Takeaways

Most stool color variations are caused by diet and are completely normal. Brown in any shade is healthy. Green from vegetables is fine. But black, red, or white stool that can't be explained by food or medication deserves medical attention.
